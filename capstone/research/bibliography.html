Annotated Bibliography

Ruzzo, Carlo, et al. “Rethinking and Comprehensive Planning of the Floating City Concept.” Communications Earth Environment, 23 Jan. 2026, www.nature.com/articles/s43247-026-03218-3, https://doi.org/10.1038/s43247-026-03218-3.
The main focus of this research article is to propose a floating city concept, spurred on by challenges with climate change and urban development. The floating city that the authors present is independent from existing coastal urban areas, and would be adaptable to a variety of locations and environmental conditions. It is composed of an inner city module of 50,000 inhabitants, and an external barrier to protect the inside from ocean waves. The writers conclude that there are methods available to ensure the floating city is physically and socially resilient, sufficient and comfortable for living, and environmentally sustainable. This article provided a lot of information to help me understand how a futuristic floating city would look and how it would function. Based on what the article says, I think advancements in floating infrastructure have potential to benefit society, especially as climate change effects ramp up.

Vegard, Aksnes and Hagbart Skage Alsos. “What Are Floating Cities and Do We Need Them?” SINTEF Blog, 10 Feb. 2023, blog.sintef.com/ocean/what-are-floating-cities-and-do-we-need-them/.
This article gives some information on floating infrastructure solutions being implemented today, and why they are necessary. The authors describe how floating infrastructure can solve space issues in areas affected by rising sea levels or areas that lack coastal space to begin with (e.g., Norway). The article explains that while the concept of fully independent floating cities out at sea is still far from being realized, floating urban districts linked to existing cities is something already being done today. The article concludes with an emphasis on how building floating cities requires a lot of expertise and various specialists to address the multidisciplinary challenges that come with making such ambitious structures. From my reading of this article, I was able to ascertain our current progression on the field of floating infrastructure, and how it could be expanded on. I believe that I could draw inspiration from current day examples like those presented in this article to create the floating homes in my project.

Warner, Andy, and Sofie Louise Dam. This Land Is My Land : A Graphic History of Big Dreams, Micronations, and Other Self-Made States. San Francisco, Chronicle Books, 2019.
This graphic novel covers various attempts to create micronations, utopias, and other idealized societies, illustrating the visionaries behind these efforts, how they actualized their dreams, and how they fell apart (or continue on). The book had some examples of floating civilizations, such as the Principality of Sealand which was based on an abandoned sea fort, that provided some inspiration for my project. It also provided some information on the issues with floating homes; such as destructive weather, or sociopolitical conflicts. The authors make it clear that these attempts are prone to failure due to having too high a scope or forces outside of the builder’s control. From what I’ve read, I’ve come to realize there are many obstacles to overcome to make a long-lasting and sustainable floating community, and will require collaborative effort to see successful results.
